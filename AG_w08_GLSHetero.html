Week08: GLS & Heteroscedasticity

Author

Tiefelsdorf

Published

March 23, 2026

1 Generalized Least Squares

  • So far, we have focused on modeling the expected value of the dependent variable with \(\mathbf{X} \cdot \boldsymbol{\beta}\) and calculated its standard error. Now we concentrate on modeling the covariance matrix of the random component.

  • Generalized Least Squares (GLS) allows addressing violations of the assumption that the regression disturbances \(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\) are independently identical distributed.

  • If we assume that the disturbances are normally distributed with \(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon} \sim N(\mathbf{0}, \sigma^2 \cdot \boldsymbol{\Omega})\) with \(\sigma^2 \cdot \boldsymbol{\Omega}\) being a covariance matrix other than \(\sigma^2 \cdot \mathbf{I}\).

  • Let us assume that the disturbances \(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\) have a covariance matrix \(\text{E}(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon} \cdot \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}^T) = \sigma^2 \cdot \boldsymbol{\Omega} \neq \sigma^2 \cdot \mathbf{I}\).

  • The generalized least squares estimator accounting for the covariance structure \(\boldsymbol{\Omega}\) in the disturbance \(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\) becomes:

\[\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}_{GLS} = (\mathbf{X}^T \cdot \boldsymbol{\Omega}^{-1} \cdot \mathbf{X})^{-1} \cdot \mathbf{X}^T \cdot \boldsymbol{\Omega}^{-1} \cdot \mathbf{y}\]

  • Alternatively, but equivalently (see Hamilton pp 188-189), after transforming the dependent variable and independent variables by \(\mathbf{y}^* = \boldsymbol{\Omega}^{-\frac{1}{2}} \cdot \mathbf{y}\) and \(\mathbf{X}^* = \boldsymbol{\Omega}^{-\frac{1}{2}} \cdot \mathbf{X}\) we get the standard OLS equation:

\[\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}_{GLS} = (\mathbf{X}^{*T} \cdot \mathbf{X}^*)^{-1} \cdot \mathbf{X}^{*T} \cdot \mathbf{y}^*\]

  • Note: For simple covariance structures \(\boldsymbol{\Omega}\), like diagonal matrices \(\boldsymbol{\Omega}\), the inverse \(\boldsymbol{\Omega}^{-1}\) or its inverse square root \(\boldsymbol{\Omega}^{-\frac{1}{2}}\) are easily calculated.

    For general covariance structures \(\boldsymbol{\Omega}\) more advanced methods such as the eigen-decomposition or Cholesky decomposition must be applied.

  • The covariance matrix of the estimated GLS regression coefficients \(\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}_{GLS}\) becomes:

\[\text{VAR}(\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}_{GLS}) = \sigma^2 \cdot (\mathbf{X}^T \cdot \boldsymbol{\Omega}^{-1} \cdot \mathbf{X})^{-1}\]

  • Why do generalized least squares estimators work?

    • The transformed regression model is: \(\boldsymbol{\Omega}^{-\frac{1}{2}} \cdot \mathbf{y} = \boldsymbol{\Omega}^{-\frac{1}{2}} \cdot \mathbf{X} \cdot \boldsymbol{\beta} + \boldsymbol{\Omega}^{-\frac{1}{2}} \cdot \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\).

    • Therefore, the transformed disturbances \(\boldsymbol{\Omega}^{-\frac{1}{2}} \cdot \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\) will follow an i.i.d normal distribution:

\[\text{E}\left(\boldsymbol{\Omega}^{-\frac{1}{2}} \cdot \boldsymbol{\varepsilon} \cdot \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}^T \cdot \boldsymbol{\Omega}^{-\frac{1}{2}^T}\right) = \boldsymbol{\Omega}^{-\frac{1}{2}} \cdot \text{E}(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon} \cdot \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}^T) \cdot \boldsymbol{\Omega}^{-\frac{1}{2}^T}\]

\[= \boldsymbol{\Omega}^{-\frac{1}{2}} \cdot (\sigma^2 \cdot \boldsymbol{\Omega}) \cdot \boldsymbol{\Omega}^{-\frac{1}{2}^T}\]

\[= \sigma^2 \cdot \mathbf{I}\]

  • The main problem is that the symmetric covariance structure \(\sigma^2 \cdot \boldsymbol{\Omega}\) of the disturbances consists of \(\frac{n \cdot (n-1)}{2} + n\) distinct components, which usually are unknown.

  • The elements of \(\sigma^2 \cdot \boldsymbol{\Omega}\) may be specified by a function based on a small set of parameters and perhaps some external information.

  • If GLS is used with a maximum likelihood estimated covariance matrix \(\hat{\boldsymbol{\Omega}}\) then it is called feasible GLS.

2 Heteroscedasticity

  • Remember, we are now explicitly modeling [a] not only the expectation \(E(\mathbf{y}) = \boldsymbol{\mu} = \mathbf{X} \cdot \boldsymbol{\beta}\) but [b] we also focus on the covariance structure among the disturbances \(\sigma^2 \cdot \boldsymbol{\Omega}\).

    Furthermore, we need the explicit assumption that the dependent variable is normally distributed \(\mathbf{y} \sim N(\boldsymbol{\mu}, \sigma^2 \cdot \boldsymbol{\Omega})\) to perform maximum likelihood estimation.

  • If heteroscedasticity is present, that is, \(\text{Var}(\varepsilon_i) = \sigma_i^2 = \sigma^2 \cdot \omega_i\), the covariance matrix takes the special diagonal form of

\[\sigma^2 \cdot \boldsymbol{\Omega} = \sigma^2 \cdot \begin{bmatrix} \omega_1 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ 0 & \omega_2 & \cdots & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & \cdots & \omega_n \end{bmatrix}\]

with its inverse and square root of its inverse being, respectively:

\[\sigma^2 \cdot \boldsymbol{\Omega}^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{\omega_1} & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ 0 & \frac{1}{\omega_2} & \cdots & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & \cdots & \frac{1}{\omega_n} \end{bmatrix} \quad \text{and} \quad \sigma^2 \cdot \boldsymbol{\Omega}^{-\frac{1}{2}} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{\sqrt{\omega_1}} & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ 0 & \frac{1}{\sqrt{\omega_2}} & \cdots & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & \cdots & \frac{1}{\sqrt{\omega_n}} \end{bmatrix}\]

, respectively.

  • Thus observations with a large variance \(\omega_i\) are weighted downward due to \(\frac{1}{\omega_i}\), because observations with large variance encompass more uncertainty.

  • The challenge becomes estimating the \(n\) unknown variances \(\text{Var}(\varepsilon_i) = \sigma_i^2 = \sigma^2 \cdot \omega_i\).

    It is impossible to estimate this many unknown variances as we have only \(n\) observations.

  • Therefore, one builds a linear functional model for the individual variances, which depends on just a few unknown parameters \(\gamma_0, \cdots, \gamma_P\) and \(P\) known variables \(\mathbf{z}_0, \cdots, \mathbf{z}_P\).

  • All \(\mathbf{z}_p\) must be positive so that the estimated variances are positive.

  • The underlying multiplicative model structure becomes:

\[\sigma_i^2 = \exp(\gamma_0 \cdot 1 + \gamma_1 \cdot \ln(z_{i1}) + \cdots + \gamma_P \cdot \ln(z_{iP})) \quad \text{with} \quad \underbrace{\sigma^2 = \exp(\gamma_0)}_{global\ variance}\]

  • It is important to note that for the function lmHetero() in the package TexMix all variable \(\mathbf{z}_p\) must be entered in their logarithmic form into the model specification.

  • For the simple model with just one variable \(\mathbf{z}_1\) expressing the heteroscedasticity we get

\[\sigma_i^2 = \exp(\gamma_0 \cdot 1 + \gamma_1 \cdot \ln(z_{i1}))\]

and if

\[\begin{aligned} \gamma_1 > 0 \quad & \sigma_i^2 \text{ is increasing with increasing } z_{i1} \\ \gamma_1 \cong 0 \quad & \sigma_i^2 \text{ is not affected by } z_{i1} \Rightarrow \text{ homoscedasticity} \\ \gamma_1 < 0 \quad & \sigma_i^2 \text{ is decreasing with increasing } z_{i1} \end{aligned}\]

  • If the estimate coefficient \(\gamma_1\) does not differ significantly for zero then the model is homoscedastic with regards to the weights variable.

  • Assuming the model is estimated with maximum likelihood, a likelihood ratio test can be performed with

\[-2 \cdot (l(OLS\ i.i.d.\ model) - l(weighted\ model)) \sim \chi^2_{df=P-1}\]

  • The R-script MultiWeightedMaxLike.r demonstrates the maximum likelihood estimation of the unknown parameter vector \((\gamma_0, \gamma_1)^T\) and the estimation of the weights vector \(\mathbf{w} = \left[\frac{1}{\omega_1}, \ldots, \frac{1}{\omega_n}\right]^T\).

  • For rates \(r_i = \frac{x_i}{n_i}\) with \(0 \leq r_i \leq 1\) and the count \(x_i\) being part of the underlying population at risk \(n_i\) we get

    • The variance of \(r_i\) is \(Var(r_i) = \frac{r_i \cdot (1-r_i)}{n_i}\) or assuming \(r_i \cdot (1 - r_i) \approx constant\) for all observations \(i\) the variance can be approximated by \(Var(r_i) = c \cdot \frac{1}{n_i} = \sigma^2 \cdot \frac{1}{n_i}\).

    • Consequently, the weights vector becomes \(= [n_1, \ldots, n_n]^T\) and we expect to have a coefficient \(\gamma_1 = -1\) because \(n_i = \left(\frac{1}{n_i}\right)^{-1}\). This implies that the variance is shrinking with an increasing population at risk \(n_i\).
